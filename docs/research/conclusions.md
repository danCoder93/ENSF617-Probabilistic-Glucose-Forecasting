# Conclusions

This report presented a probabilistic glucose forecasting pipeline built on the AZT1D dataset and implemented as a late fusion model that combines three TCN branches with a TFT branch. The data pipeline preserves a clear distinction between static context, known future covariates, observed history, and target history, and the saved run confirms that the full system can train end to end and emit coherent quantile forecasts over a one hour horizon.

Empirically, the current baseline is strongest at short horizons and weakest in low glucose conditions. Overall MAE is `20.17 mg/dL`, coverage of the central `80%` interval is `76.1%`, and performance degrades steadily toward the end of the horizon, where MAE reaches `28.35 mg/dL`. The most important weakness is the low glucose regime, where the model overpredicts strongly and interval coverage falls to `20.2%`. These findings support the present system as a useful research baseline, but not yet as a reliable forecasting model for clinically sensitive settings.

The next stage of the project should focus on longer and better controlled training, improved calibration, stronger handling of rare low glucose events, and clearer comparison against simpler baselines and subject held out evaluation settings. Those experiments will determine whether the fused TCN and TFT design offers a real empirical advantage beyond being a well organized starting point.
